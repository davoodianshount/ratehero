Glossary

Refinance

Replacing an existing mortgage with a new one — typically to lower the rate, change loan terms, or extract equity from the property.

What is a refinance?

A refinance replaces your existing mortgage with a new one on the same property. The new loan pays off the old loan, and you continue making payments on the new terms. Refinancing isn't about getting "extra" money in the simple case — it's about restructuring your existing debt to better fit your current situation.

For real estate investors, refinancing is one of the most powerful portfolio management tools. Done well, it can lower monthly payments, extract equity for new acquisitions, switch loan products (e.g., from hard money to DSCR), or restructure debt across multiple properties. Done poorly, it can reset amortization clocks, trigger prepayment penalties, and cost more than it saves.

The four main types of refinance

Investors typically deal with four refinance types, each with different goals:

Rate-and-term refinance. Replace your existing mortgage with a new one at a better interest rate, different term length, or different loan type — without taking cash out. Total new loan amount is roughly the same as the existing loan balance. Goal: lower monthly payment or change loan structure. See rate-and-term refinance.

Cash-out refinance. Replace your existing mortgage with a larger one and receive the difference in cash. Goal: extract built-up equity for use elsewhere — typically funding the next acquisition or capital improvements. See cash-out refinance.

Streamline refinance. A simplified refinance available on certain government-backed loans (FHA, VA) that reduces documentation requirements. Doesn't apply to most investor properties since DSCR loans are not government-backed. Available for owner-occupied or qualified investment scenarios with existing FHA/VA loans.

Bridge-to-permanent refinance. Replace short-term hard money or bridge financing with long-term DSCR or conventional financing. This is the "R" in BRRRR strategy — buy/rehab with hard money, then refinance into permanent financing once the property is stabilized.

The math: when refinancing makes sense

The classic break-even calculation: total closing costs of the refinance divided by monthly savings equals the break-even period. If closing costs are $6,000 and you save $200/month with the new rate, your break-even is 30 months — you need to hold the property at least 2.5 years to come out ahead.

For investors, the math is more complex than just rate savings:

1. Prepayment penalty on existing loan. If you're still within the prepayment penalty period on your DSCR loan, the penalty often eliminates the refinance savings. See prepayment penalty.

2. Amortization reset. Refinancing into a new 30-year loan in year 5 of your current loan means restarting the amortization clock. Your monthly payment includes more interest and less principal than you'd be paying without the refinance. See amortization.

3. New origination and closing costs. Each refinance carries $4,000-$10,000+ in closing costs depending on loan size — appraisal, title insurance, origination fees, recording fees, attorney fees in some states.

4. Cash-out impact on DSCR. If you're doing a cash-out refinance, the larger loan amount means higher PITIA, which lowers your DSCR. The new loan must still cover its own debt service from rental income.

The "right" refinance moment balances all of these. A simple rate-and-term refinance with no prepayment penalty and a 1%+ rate drop typically breaks even in 12-24 months and is a clear win. A cash-out refinance with a 5-4-3-2-1 prepayment penalty in year 2 might cost more than it generates over the next 3 years.

DSCR refinances: what's different

Refinancing into a DSCR loan follows the same underwriting principles as a purchase DSCR loan — qualifying on rental income rather than personal income, vested in an LLC, no tax returns required. Several specific considerations apply to DSCR refinances:

Seasoning requirements. Most DSCR lenders require 3-6 months of ownership before you can refinance — sometimes longer for cash-out (6-12 months). This prevents the "buy at 60% LTV, immediately refi at 80% LTV to extract value" tactic that lenders consider too risky.

The 1007 rent schedule controls. Your DSCR is calculated using the appraiser's 1007 estimated rent, not your actual lease. If your tenant pays $2,500/month but the 1007 estimates $2,200/month for that area, your DSCR is based on $2,200. This sometimes blocks refinances on properties with strong actual rent but moderate market rent.

LLC continuity. If the property is currently vested in your personal name but the new DSCR loan must vest in an LLC, you'll need to deed it to your LLC first. This can complicate the refinance and may have tax implications.

Prepayment penalty on the new loan. Refinancing into a new DSCR loan typically resets the prepayment penalty clock. New 5-4-3-2-1 starts again. Factor this into refinance timing if you're planning a future cash-out or sale.

Common investor refinance scenarios

BRRRR refinance (bridge → DSCR). You bought a property with hard money in cash or with bridge financing, renovated it, and now have it rented at market rate. Refinance into a DSCR loan based on the new appraised value (typically 75-80% LTV). Cash extracted often pays off the original hard money loan AND returns some equity for the next deal. This is the canonical BRRRR exit.

Rate reduction refinance (DSCR → DSCR). Rates dropped 1% since you closed. You're past the prepayment penalty period or willing to pay it. Refinance into a new DSCR at lower rate. Common when rates fall meaningfully.

Cash-out for next acquisition. Property has appreciated 30% since purchase, rents are strong, and you want to fund another acquisition. Cash-out refinance pulls out equity (typically up to 75% LTV) while keeping the property in your portfolio.

Switch from DSCR to conventional. Your personal financial situation has improved (more documented income, lower DTI), and conventional financing now qualifies. Conventional rates are typically lower than DSCR. Refinancing from DSCR to conventional saves money if you can qualify.

Switch from conventional to DSCR. Your personal DTI has hit conventional limits (too many properties to qualify for the next loan). Refinance one or more properties from conventional to DSCR to free up DTI capacity for the next conventional loan.

What gets refinanced: the closing process

A refinance closing is similar to a purchase closing but with the existing lender as the "seller" being paid off:

Loan application and approval. Same documentation as a purchase loan. Income/DSCR verification, appraisal, title work, underwriting.

Closing costs. Appraisal, title insurance (new policy required even on refinance), recording fees, origination fees, lender attorney (in some states). Total typically $4,000-$10,000+.

Payoff of existing loan. The closing settlement pays off your existing mortgage in full from the new loan proceeds. Any cash-out amount above the existing payoff goes to you (or the LLC).

Recording the new mortgage. County records the new mortgage, releasing the old one. You now have a new mortgage with new terms, payment schedule, and (usually) new servicer.

Rate Hero's take

For investor clients, the question isn't usually "should I refinance" — it's "when, and into what?"

Three principles we apply when evaluating refinance opportunities:

Calculate the all-in cost, not just rate savings. Prepayment penalty on existing loan + closing costs on new loan + amortization reset penalty must all be subtracted from gross rate savings to get true net benefit.

Don't refinance for the sake of refinancing. A 0.25% rate drop typically isn't worth a refinance once closing costs are factored in. A 0.5%+ drop, paired with no prepayment penalty, usually is.

Bundle strategic moves when possible. If you're refinancing anyway, ask whether to do cash-out at the same time. A standalone cash-out a year later costs another full round of closing costs. If you have a plan for the cash, do it once.

For BRRRR investors specifically, the refinance is the entire strategy. The discipline isn't whether to refinance — it's making sure you bought right, renovated right, and have the rents to support the refinance you planned. We see BRRRR investors fail at the refinance stage when initial purchase price was too high or rents didn't materialize. The refinance reveals every prior mistake.

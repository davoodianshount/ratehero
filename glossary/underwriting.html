Glossary

Underwriting

The lender's process of evaluating your loan application — verifying income, assets, credit, and the property — to decide whether to approve, deny, or condition the loan.

What is underwriting?

Underwriting is the formal process where a lender evaluates whether to approve your mortgage. After you submit a loan application with supporting documents, an underwriter reviews everything — your finances, the property, the loan terms — and decides whether to issue the loan, deny it, or approve it subject to specific conditions you must meet before closing.

For real estate investors, underwriting is often the slowest and most frustrating part of a deal. But understanding what underwriters actually check — and how different loan products approach underwriting differently — turns underwriting from a black box into a manageable process.

What underwriters evaluate

Every underwriter looks at the same general categories, though the depth varies dramatically by loan type:

The borrower. Credit history, credit score, employment or income source, financial reserves, and prior real estate experience. Conventional loans go deep here. DSCR loans go light.

The property. Appraised value, condition, location, and (for investment properties) rental income potential. All loan types evaluate the property, but DSCR loans put more weight here than on the borrower.

The loan structure. Loan-to-value ratio, down payment source, loan term, and rate type. Some loan structures introduce additional risk requiring more documentation.

Title and ownership. Verifying that the seller actually owns the property and can convey clean title. Handled through the title company but reviewed by the underwriter.

Conventional underwriting: documentation-heavy

Conventional underwriting (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac standards) is heavily focused on the borrower's personal income and financial profile. Required documentation typically includes:

Two years of tax returns (personal and business). Two years of W-2s. Most recent 30 days of pay stubs. Two months of bank statements for all asset accounts. Verification of employment letter. Explanation letters for any credit issues, large deposits, or employment gaps. Proof of source of down payment funds. Documentation of any other real estate owned (REO schedule).

For self-employed investors, this expands dramatically — profit and loss statements, business bank statements, year-to-date P&Ls, and often CPA letters. The documentation burden is one reason conventional underwriting frequently takes 30-45 days.

Conventional underwriters use a debt-to-income ratio (DTI) cap, typically 43-45% maximum. They count every monthly obligation against your gross monthly income. For investors with multiple properties, this DTI math gets complex fast — and is often what disqualifies otherwise-qualified investors from conventional financing.

DSCR underwriting: property-focused

DSCR underwriting flips the model. Instead of qualifying the borrower's personal income, the underwriter qualifies the property's cash flow. The core question: does the property's rental income cover its mortgage payment plus taxes and insurance (PITIA)?

The simplified DSCR calculation: rent ÷ PITIA. A DSCR of 1.20 means rent covers PITIA with 20% headroom. Most DSCR programs require DSCR of 1.0 minimum (some allow sub-1.0 DSCR, others require 1.25+).

What DSCR underwriters typically still check:

Credit score. Minimum 660-680 for most programs. Higher scores unlock better pricing.

Reserves. 3-6 months of PITIA in liquid reserves after closing. See reserves.

Property condition. Via the appraisal. Properties needing significant rehab don't qualify for standard DSCR loans (you'd need a bridge or hard money loan instead).

Title and entity structure. DSCR loans typically vest in an LLC. The underwriter reviews LLC formation documents to verify ownership.

What DSCR underwriters generally DON'T require:

Tax returns. W-2s. Pay stubs. Employment verification. Debt-to-income calculations. This is what makes DSCR loans so much faster than conventional — typically 21 days from application to closing vs. 30-45 for conventional.

The three underwriting outcomes

An underwriter's decision falls into one of three categories:

Approved. Loan is approved as submitted. No additional conditions. Move to closing. This is rare — almost every loan has at least minor conditions.

Approved with conditions. Loan is approved subject to meeting specific conditions before closing. Common conditions include: updated bank statements, explanation letters for specific items, lease agreement copies, hazard insurance binders, signed disclosures, additional documentation of asset source. This is the typical outcome.

Denied. Loan cannot be approved as submitted. Reasons vary — credit issues, insufficient income/DSCR, property condition, title issues, or appraisal coming in too low.

Common conditions and how to clear them fast

Conditions are usually clearable in 24-48 hours if you know what's coming. The most common conditions on DSCR loans:

Updated bank statements. If your initial statements are over 60 days old by underwriting, you'll need updated ones. Have your most recent statements ready.

Hazard insurance binder. The lender requires proof of property insurance starting on the closing date. Order your insurance early (most lenders won't approve final docs without it).

Lease agreement for existing tenant. If buying a property with a tenant in place, the underwriter needs the current lease agreement to confirm rental income matches the 1007 estimate.

LLC documentation. Operating agreement, articles of organization, EIN letter from IRS. Have these ready before applying if vesting in an LLC.

Source of funds letter. If your down payment came from a large recent deposit (not from steady savings), the underwriter wants documentation showing where those funds came from. Best practice: avoid large unexplained deposits in the 60 days before applying.

HOA documents. For condo or PUD purchases, the underwriter needs HOA financials, master insurance policy, and bylaws. These come from the HOA management company.

What slows down underwriting

The top reasons underwriting drags past expected timelines:

Document delays. The underwriter asks for something, the borrower takes a week to provide it. This is the #1 cause of delays. Stay responsive — turn around requests within 24 hours.

Appraisal issues. A low appraisal or appraisal scheduling delays push everything back. See appraisal.

Title issues. Unexpected liens, ownership disputes, or unclear chain of title. The title company handles these but the loan can't close until cleared.

Conditions stacking. Clearing one condition reveals a new question that triggers another condition. Common with complex borrower profiles or LLC structures.

Insurance issues. Some properties are hard to insure (older roofs, flood zones, properties with prior claims). Insurance underwriting runs parallel to mortgage underwriting and can become the bottleneck.

Rate Hero's take

For investor clients, underwriting velocity is often the difference between winning and losing a deal. Cash buyers can close in 7-14 days; competitive DSCR financing closes in 21 days. Slower than that, and you're losing offers to faster competition.

Three principles that consistently speed up underwriting:

Front-load documentation. Provide everything the underwriter is likely to need on day one — even before they ask. Tax returns ready if needed, asset statements ready, LLC docs ready, prior property leases ready.

Stay responsive. Treat the underwriter like a same-day client. Conditions sent on Monday should be cleared by Tuesday, not by next Monday.

Know your loan officer's process. Some loan officers act as a buffer between borrower and underwriter; others put you in direct contact. Direct contact is usually faster. Ask your loan officer how they handle conditions and respond accordingly.

Underwriting feels like a black box from the outside. From the inside, it's a fairly predictable sequence — and predictable sequences can be optimized.

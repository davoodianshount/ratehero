Glossary

Mortgage Points

Upfront fees paid to a lender at closing — discount points lower your interest rate, while origination points compensate the lender for processing the loan.

What are mortgage points?

Mortgage points are upfront fees paid to the lender at closing, calculated as a percentage of the loan amount. One point equals 1% of the loan. On a $400,000 loan, one point costs $4,000 at closing. Two points cost $8,000. The dollar amount scales linearly with loan size.

The confusion most borrowers have: "points" can refer to two completely different things — discount points (which you choose to pay) and origination points (which the lender charges). They behave differently and have different strategic implications. Understanding the distinction is critical for evaluating loan offers.

Discount points: buying down your rate

Discount points are optional. You choose whether to pay them. The deal is straightforward: pay the lender money upfront, and they reduce your interest rate for the life of the loan.

Standard pricing: one discount point (1% of loan amount) typically reduces the interest rate by 0.25%. The exact ratio varies by lender and loan product, but 1 point for 0.25% is the rough industry standard.

Example on a $400,000 loan:

No points: 7.50% rate, $4,000 less at closing, $2,797 monthly payment

1 point ($4,000 paid): 7.25% rate, $2,728 monthly payment ($69/month savings)

2 points ($8,000 paid): 7.00% rate, $2,661 monthly payment ($136/month savings)

The math question: is paying $4,000 upfront worth $69/month in savings? That depends on how long you hold the loan.

Discount point breakeven math

The breakeven calculation is simple: divide the upfront cost by the monthly savings to find how many months until you've recovered the cost.

$4,000 ÷ $69/month = 58 months (4.8 years)

If you hold the loan more than 4.8 years, you come out ahead by buying the point. If you sell or refinance before 4.8 years, you've spent $4,000 to get less than $4,000 in savings.

For investor properties, this breakeven matters because:

BRRRR investors typically refinance within 12-18 months. Buying down a hard money rate by paying points rarely makes sense — you'll refinance before the breakeven hits.

Long-term hold investors (10+ years) almost always benefit from discount points. The compounding savings over a decade significantly exceed upfront cost.

Mid-hold investors (3-7 years) face the closest decision. Calculate the specific breakeven against your most realistic hold timeline.

The advanced math: what discount points actually return

Beyond simple breakeven, discount points can be evaluated as an investment with a specific return rate.

On the $400,000 loan example above: you "invest" $4,000 (the point cost) and receive $69/month in savings ($828/year). That's roughly a 20.7% annual return on the $4,000 investment for as long as you hold the loan.

Compared to other places to deploy $4,000 — paying down higher-interest debt, investing in stocks, holding cash — the 20.7% effective return on discount points is attractive IF you'll hold the loan long enough.

The catch: that return only materializes through monthly savings over time. The capital is locked in the loan structure. You can't sell discount points or recover them by paying off the loan early.

Origination points: lender compensation

Origination points are different. They're not optional — they're how the lender gets paid for processing the loan. Like discount points, they're calculated as a percentage of the loan amount, but they don't reduce your interest rate. They just compensate the lender.

Some lenders advertise "no origination points" — meaning their compensation comes through the interest rate (lender-paid compensation via slightly higher rate). Some charge 0.5-1.5 points in origination fees with slightly lower rates. Same total cost to you, different fee structure.

This matters when comparing loan offers. A "0 points" loan from Lender A might have a higher rate than a "1 point origination" loan from Lender B. Calculate the APR to compare apples-to-apples — see APR.

Lender credits: negative points

The opposite of discount points: lender credits, sometimes called "negative points." The lender gives you a credit at closing (reducing your closing costs) in exchange for accepting a slightly higher interest rate.

Example: instead of 7.25% rate with $0 lender credit, you take 7.50% rate with $4,000 lender credit toward closing costs. You pay less at closing but more monthly.

This makes sense in three scenarios:

Tight closing budget. If you need to minimize cash at closing, lender credits help.

Short expected hold. If you'll sell or refinance quickly, the higher rate matters less than the upfront savings.

Refinance economics. Refinance closing costs typically don't generate the same long-term equity benefit as purchase closing costs. Lender credits can absorb refinance fees without long-term impact.

Tax treatment of points

For investment properties, mortgage points generally must be amortized over the loan's term rather than deducted in the year paid. On a 30-year loan with $4,000 in discount points, you typically deduct approximately $133/year over 30 years rather than $4,000 in year one.

For primary residences, IRS rules allow deducting discount points in full in the year paid (subject to specific conditions). Investment property treatment is different — consult a tax advisor for your specific situation.

This tax treatment difference matters when evaluating discount points on investment property. Pure cash-flow analysis (without tax considerations) usually shows points making sense for long holds. Adding amortization rather than year-one deduction shifts the math slightly.

When discount points typically make sense for investors

Three scenarios where buying down the rate is usually worth it:

Long-term buy-and-hold (10+ year horizon). The savings compound over many years, dwarfing upfront cost. Almost always positive expected value.

DSCR loans with marginal cash flow. Lower rate improves DSCR coverage. A property that pencils at 1.05 DSCR without points might be 1.15 DSCR with 1 point paid down. Stronger DSCR sometimes unlocks better loan tiers or higher LTV.

Properties in tight cash-flow markets. California coastal, parts of Florida, premium urban properties. Every basis point of rate savings matters when the margin is thin.

When discount points usually don't make sense

Three scenarios where you should skip the points:

BRRRR refinance candidate. If you'll refinance within 2-3 years anyway (BRRRR exit, conventional-to-DSCR transition, rate-and-term refi), paying points on the current loan is wasted capital.

Unknown hold timeline. If you don't know whether you'll hold 3 years or 15 years, the uncertainty argues against locking up capital in points. Keep cash flexible.

High-cash-flow markets. In markets with strong rents relative to property prices, the extra 0.25% of rate savings is small relative to your monthly cash flow. Deploy the capital into your next acquisition instead.

Rate Hero's take

For investor clients, the discount points decision is rarely about "should I buy down the rate" — it's about "where does this $4,000 produce the best return?"

Your alternatives for the capital include: holding more reserves, accelerating the next acquisition, capital improvements to the current property, paying down higher-interest debt, or other investments. The discount point return needs to compete against all of these.

Three quick screening questions before paying discount points:

Will I hold this loan past breakeven? Calculate the specific breakeven months. If your honest hold horizon is shorter, skip the points.

Could that cash fund another deal sooner? $4,000 doesn't fund a full down payment, but $20,000 in points across multiple properties does. Capital deployed in points isn't available for the next acquisition.

What's my opportunity cost? If you could earn 8% deploying the capital elsewhere, the points need to beat that net of taxes and risk.

The default for most growth-phase investors: skip discount points, deploy capital into the next acquisition instead. The default for consolidation-phase investors: pay points on properties you'll hold permanently, optimize for cash flow over portfolio expansion.

Match your point strategy to your portfolio strategy. The math works for both directions depending on which phase you're in.

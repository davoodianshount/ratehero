Glossary

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)

The annual cost of a loan expressed as a percentage — including the interest rate plus origination fees, points, mortgage insurance, and other recurring loan costs.

What is APR?

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the total annual cost of a loan expressed as a percentage. It bundles together the interest rate plus most of the loan's fees and recurring costs into a single number designed to enable easy comparison between competing loan offers.

If you've ever wondered why your loan estimate shows two different rates — say, 7.25% interest rate and 7.43% APR — the gap is the fees and costs the lender is charging. APR captures both. Interest rate alone captures only the cost of the borrowed money.

How APR differs from interest rate

The interest rate is what you pay annually for borrowing the principal balance. If you borrow $400,000 at 7.5%, you're charged 7.5% of the outstanding balance each year, divided into monthly payments. The interest rate determines your principal-and-interest payment.

APR includes the interest rate PLUS most other recurring loan costs:

Origination fees. Charges the lender bills for processing the loan.

Discount points. Upfront fees paid to lower the interest rate.

Mortgage insurance (PMI/MIP). Ongoing insurance premiums on high-LTV loans.

Underwriting fees. Some lenders include these in APR calculations.

Mortgage broker compensation. If you're working with a broker.

APR does NOT typically include:

Title insurance. Separate from the loan itself.

Appraisal fees. Typically excluded from APR.

Recording fees. Government charges, not lender charges.

Hazard insurance. Property insurance, separate from the loan.

The result: APR is always equal to or higher than the interest rate. A loan with zero fees and no points would have an APR equal to its interest rate. A loan with significant origination fees or discount points will have a noticeably higher APR.

Why APR exists: the Truth in Lending Act

APR is a federal regulatory creation. The Truth in Lending Act (TILA), passed in 1968, requires lenders to disclose APR on every consumer loan offer. The purpose: prevent lenders from hiding fees behind low advertised interest rates.

Before APR existed, lenders could advertise "5% interest rate" while burying $15,000 in origination fees in the closing costs. Consumers couldn't easily compare offers because the fee structures varied so widely. APR forces all those costs into a single comparable number.

For investment property loans, including DSCR loans, APR disclosure rules vary. Some commercial-purpose investor loans aren't subject to TILA, so APR may not be required on the disclosure. Always ask for it explicitly when shopping loans — even if not legally required, lenders typically calculate and provide it on request.

The APR-to-rate gap: what it tells you

The gap between APR and interest rate signals the loan's fee load:

Small gap (under 0.15%). Low fees and minimal points. The advertised rate is roughly what you actually pay.

Medium gap (0.15-0.40%). Standard fee load. Most market-rate loans land here.

Large gap (0.40%+). Heavy fee structure, possibly with discount points buying down the rate. The advertised rate is meaningfully lower than your true cost.

Example: Two competing offers on the same $400,000 loan:

Lender A: 7.25% interest rate, 7.55% APR (0.30% gap). Standard fee load.

Lender B: 7.00% interest rate, 7.75% APR (0.75% gap). The lower rate is bought down with heavy points. APR reveals Lender A is actually cheaper despite the higher headline rate.

This is precisely why APR exists — to prevent the "lowest rate" comparison trap.

APR limitations to understand

APR is a useful comparison tool but has real limitations:

APR assumes you hold the loan to term. Origination fees and discount points are amortized over the full 30 years in APR calculations. If you sell or refinance in year 3, those upfront costs were paid for 27 years of benefit you'll never receive. APR will overstate the actual cost in that scenario.

APR doesn't capture all loan terms. Two loans with identical APRs might have wildly different prepayment penalties, rate adjustment caps (on ARMs), or loan covenants. APR is one factor, not the complete picture.

APR varies by calculation method. Different lenders may include slightly different fees in their APR calculations. Federal law sets minimum requirements, but interpretations vary. A 0.05% APR difference between lenders may reflect calculation methodology, not actual cost difference.

Investment property loan APRs may not include all relevant costs. For non-QM and DSCR loans, some fees specific to investor financing (entity setup costs, asset verification fees, prepayment structures) may sit outside APR calculations.

How to use APR for investor loan shopping

Three practical applications:

Compare loan offers within the same loan type. Comparing two DSCR loans on the same property at the same LTV, APR is the right comparison tool. Same product, different lenders, APR cuts through marketing noise.

Don't compare APR across loan types. A 30-year fixed DSCR APR doesn't directly compare to a 5/1 ARM APR or a hard money APR. Different products serve different needs. Compare like products.

Calculate break-even on points. If a lender offers a lower rate with discount points, the APR helps but doesn't completely answer "should I buy down the rate?" That's a function of your hold timeline — see discount points.

Rate Hero's take

For investor clients, APR is a useful starting point for comparing offers but not the final word.

The most expensive mistake we see: investors comparing only the headline interest rate across competing lenders. The "best rate" often comes with the worst fee structure. APR catches this immediately.

The second most common mistake: investors over-weighting APR when their actual hold horizon is short. If you're refinancing or selling within 3-5 years, APR (which assumes 30-year hold) overweights upfront fees. In those cases, focus on total dollars paid over your actual hold period — not the 30-year APR.

Use APR for first-cut comparison. Then look at the specific fee schedule, prepayment terms, and rate adjustment provisions for the final decision. APR is the entry point; the loan estimate is the full picture.

Glossary

FICO Score

A credit score ranging from 300 to 850, calculated by Fair Isaac Corporation, that lenders use to evaluate creditworthiness on mortgage applications.

What is a FICO score?

A FICO score is a three-digit number between 300 and 850 that represents your creditworthiness — a lender's estimate of how likely you are to repay borrowed money on time. Higher numbers mean lower risk to lenders, which translates to better loan terms.

FICO is one specific brand of credit score, developed by Fair Isaac Corporation. Other credit scores exist (VantageScore is the main competitor), but FICO is the score most mortgage lenders actually use. When someone says "credit score" in a mortgage context, they almost always mean FICO score.

The FICO score range tiers

FICO scores break down into general tiers that affect loan eligibility and pricing:

800-850: Exceptional. Top tier. Best loan terms available, lowest rates, easiest approval. Roughly 20% of US consumers fall here.

740-799: Very Good. Strong tier. Most loan products are accessible with competitive pricing. Most investor borrowers should target this range.

670-739: Good. Acceptable for most loans. Pricing slightly worse than the higher tiers. Still qualifies for conventional and DSCR financing.

580-669: Fair. Higher risk to lenders. Conventional loans may be unavailable or carry higher rates. FHA loans typically still accessible (FHA requires 580+ for 3.5% down). DSCR programs vary — some require 660+ minimum.

300-579: Poor. Most conventional and investor loan products are unavailable. Hard money or specialty products may be the only options, with significantly higher rates.

How FICO scores are calculated

FICO scores weight five factors with specific percentages:

Payment history (35%). The biggest factor. On-time payments build score; missed payments destroy it. A single 30-day-late mortgage payment can drop a FICO score 60-110 points depending on starting score.

Credit utilization (30%). The percentage of available credit you're using. Keeping balances under 30% of credit limits is ideal; under 10% is optimal for top-tier scores. This is the fastest lever to improve a FICO score — paying down credit cards before a credit pull can lift scores 20-40 points in 30 days.

Length of credit history (15%). How long you've had credit accounts open. Average age of accounts matters. Closing old accounts shortens your history and can hurt your score.

Credit mix (10%). Variety of credit types — credit cards, installment loans, mortgages. Having only credit cards weighs less favorably than having a mix.

New credit inquiries (10%). Hard inquiries from credit applications. Each hard inquiry typically drops FICO 5-10 points temporarily. Multiple inquiries within 30 days for the same loan type (e.g., mortgage shopping) usually count as one inquiry.

How FICO affects investor loan pricing

FICO score directly affects what you'll pay on a mortgage. The impact is meaningful:

DSCR loans. Most DSCR programs require minimum 660-680 FICO. Pricing tiers typically break at 700, 720, 740, and 760. Moving from 700 to 760 can save 0.25-0.50% on rate — meaningful over 30 years.

Conventional investment loans. Minimum 620 typically, with significant pricing breaks at 680, 720, 740, and 760. The spread between 620 and 760 can be 1.0%+ on rate, costing tens of thousands over the loan's life.

FHA loans. 580 minimum for 3.5% down, 500-579 with 10% down. Rate pricing relatively flat above 620 (FHA loans are insured by HUD, so credit risk passes to the government).

Hard money and bridge loans. Credit-light products. Many lenders accept 600-660 minimum because the loans are secured primarily by the property's after-repair value. Rate impact from credit score is smaller than on conventional/DSCR.

The three-bureau model

FICO scores come from three credit bureaus: Experian, Equifax, and TransUnion. Each bureau maintains its own credit file and produces its own FICO score. Your three scores are usually close but rarely identical because different creditors report to different bureaus.

For mortgage applications, lenders typically pull all three scores and use the middle score for qualification. If your scores are 740, 752, and 768, the lender uses 752. This protects against single-bureau errors while not over-rewarding the highest single score.

Practical implication: improving credit isn't enough to fix one bureau — you need consistent improvement across all three. Disputing errors only with one bureau leaves the same issue affecting your middle score from another bureau.

Managing FICO across multiple investor acquisitions

Investors acquiring multiple properties face a specific challenge: each loan application triggers credit pulls, and each new mortgage affects future applications.

Mortgage shopping windows. FICO allows mortgage rate-shopping inquiries within a 14-45 day window to count as one inquiry (varies by FICO model version). Apply with multiple lenders within 30 days to minimize score impact.

New mortgage debt impact. Each new mortgage adds to your overall debt burden, affecting credit utilization metrics on the next application. The impact stabilizes after 6-12 months of on-time payments.

DSCR loans don't affect personal DTI but DO affect personal credit. While DSCR underwriting doesn't count your other rental debts against your DTI for qualification, the mortgages still appear on your personal credit report and affect FICO calculations.

Timing strategies. Investors planning multiple acquisitions often submit applications in batches with cooldown periods between batches, allowing FICO scores to stabilize before the next round.

How to improve FICO score for upcoming applications

Five tactics that move FICO scores meaningfully:

Pay down credit card balances. Most impactful short-term move. Reducing utilization from 40% to under 10% can lift scores 20-50 points in 30 days. Pay before statement closing date so lower balance reports to bureaus.

Dispute credit report errors. Review reports from all three bureaus annually. Incorrect late payments, accounts you don't own, or duplicate accounts can suppress your score 50+ points. Disputes typically resolve in 30-45 days.

Don't close old credit cards. Even unused cards contribute to length of credit history and available credit (which lowers utilization). Closing old cards can drop scores 20-40 points.

Pay every bill on time, every month. Even a single late payment harms your score for 24+ months. Set up autopay on minimum payments to prevent accidental misses.

Limit new credit applications before mortgage shopping. Don't open new credit cards, car loans, or store accounts within 6 months of a planned mortgage application. Each hard inquiry costs points you can't easily get back.

Rate Hero's take

For investor clients, FICO management is portfolio infrastructure — just like cash reserves or insurance coverage. Treating credit as something you check once before applying is the wrong mental model.

Three principles we apply with investor clients:

Monitor monthly, not annually. Free credit monitoring services flag changes immediately. You can't fix what you don't see.

Strategic credit decisions need 6-month lead time. Planning to acquire next quarter? Pay down balances this quarter. Major moves take 60-90 days to fully reflect.

Don't optimize for a single application — optimize for the next 5 years. Investors will apply for 5-15 loans over the next decade. Building and maintaining 740+ FICO across that period saves orders of magnitude more than tactical score-pumping before any single application.

The good news: FICO management is one of the few investor success factors fully under your control. Property markets move. Interest rates move. Tenant quality varies. Your credit score is something you can build and protect through discipline alone.

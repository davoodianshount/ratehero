Conventional loans remain the most common mortgage product for borrowers with strong credit and documented income. This guide covers everything you need to know about conventional loans for 2-4 unit properties — what you need to know in 2026.

What Makes Conventional Loans Different

Conventional loans are not government-backed — they conform to Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines, which means they follow standardized underwriting rules around income, credit, and down payment. The result: competitive rates for qualified borrowers and flexible use cases that government loans often don't allow.

Key Requirements

To qualify for a conventional loan, borrowers typically need:

  • Credit score: 620 minimum, 740+ for best pricing
  • Down payment: As low as 3% on primary residences (PMI required below 20%)
  • DTI ratio: Maximum 45–50% depending on compensating factors
  • Income documentation: 2 years W-2s, 1 month pay stubs, 2 months bank statements

Conventional vs Government Loan Programs

Unlike FHA loans, conventional loans don't require mortgage insurance for the life of the loan. Once you reach 20% equity, PMI can be removed. For investment properties, conventional loans (up to 10 financed properties per Fannie Mae guidelines) are often the first choice for W-2 borrowers before they transition to DSCR financing.

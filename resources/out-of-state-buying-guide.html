Why Buy Out of State
THE CASE
FOR BUYING
ELSEWHERE.
Prices in your backyard don't dictate your returns. The best cash-flowing markets are rarely where the most expensive people live.
The Core Reason
Your Home Market May Be Priced Out
A $700K single-family in Los Angeles rents for $3,200/month — a DSCR below 0.6. That same $700K in Columbus, Ohio buys three $230K rentals each cash-flowing at $1,400/month. The math doesn't care where you live.
The Homebuyer Case
Moving to a Lower Cost-of-Living State
Relocating from California, New York, or New Jersey to Tennessee, Florida, or Texas? You're buying in a market where your dollar buys 2–3x more home — and your financing options are the same.

DSCR Out of State — How It Works
The Property Qualifies Itself — Wherever It Is
DSCR loans are perfectly designed for out-of-state investing. You qualify based on the property's rent — not your job, income, or location. A California buyer with no Ohio income history qualifies the exact same way as a local Ohio investor: on the lease or Form 1007 market rent.

Remote Due Diligence
BUYING A
HOME YOU'VE
NEVER SEEN.
This is the part that scares most first-time out-of-state buyers. It shouldn't. Done right, remote due diligence is thorough, efficient, and totally normal in 2025.
Your Remote Due Diligence Team
You Need Four People on the Ground
1
A buyer's agent who specializes in investor purchases — not just residential sales. Ask specifically about out-of-state buyer experience. They should be doing video walkthroughs proactively.
2
A licensed inspector — never skip this out of state. You especially cannot afford to discover a foundation issue after you've already closed remotely. Pay for the thorough report.
3
A local property manager — interview them before you buy, not after. They know actual rental rates, tenant quality, and neighborhood dynamics better than any Zillow estimate.
4
A title company or real estate attorney — some states require attorneys at closing (attorney states). Your lender and agent can confirm which applies to your target state.

Closing Process — Attorney States vs Title States
Some States Require a Real Estate Attorney at Closing
In attorney states, a licensed attorney must be present at closing. In title states, a title company handles it. Both work fine — you just need to budget for it and know who to hire.

06
What Goes Wrong
MISTAKES
OUT-OF-STATE
BUYERS MAKE.
Most of these are avoidable. All of them are expensive. Read this before you make an offer.
Mistake 01
Using Zillow Rent Estimates for DSCR Math
Zillow rent estimates are often 8–15% high in investor-heavy markets. If you underwrite at $1,600/month and the appraiser's 1007 comes back at $1,380, your DSCR drops and the deal may not qualify. Always use local PM comps or wait for the 1007.
Mistake 02
Not Accounting for Property Management
You cannot self-manage a property 2,000 miles away. PM fees run 8–12% of rent. If that $1,400/month rental costs $140–$168 in PM fees, that's money that comes directly out of your cash flow. Run the numbers with PM costs included.
Mistake 03
Skipping the Inspection Because You Can't Be There
This is where most money gets lost. A $500 inspection that finds a $22,000 foundation issue is the best money you'll ever spend. Never waive inspection on an out-of-state purchase. Have your agent attend on your behalf and FaceTime through it.
Mistake 04
Using Your Home State Lender Who Isn't Licensed There
Your local bank may not be licensed to lend in Ohio. Discovering this at week three of a 30-day escrow is a serious problem. Confirm your lender is licensed in the property state before you go under contract. Rate Hero is Available in 30+ states.
Mistake 05
Ignoring Local Landlord Law
Eviction timelines vary dramatically. Indiana: 20–30 days. New York: 3–6 months. If your cash flow model assumes quick tenant turnover in a tenant-friendly state, your vacancy math is wrong. Research eviction law in your target state.
Mistake 06
Not Building a Reserves Buffer
Out-of-state properties need a larger reserves cushion than local ones — you can't drive over and fix something yourself. Budget for 3–6 months of PITIA in reserves and a separate maintenance fund. 6 months is not conservative — it's standard for remote ownership.

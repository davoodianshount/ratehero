Step 01 · Before You Talk to Anyone
GET YOUR
FINANCES
IN ORDER.
Most buyers skip this and pay for it later. Do this before you apply for a single thing.
Credit
Pull Your Full Credit Report
Check all three bureaus — Equifax, Experian, TransUnion. Dispute any errors before you apply. This takes time and can take weeks to resolve. Don't wait until you're under contract.
Debt Ratio
Calculate Your DTI
Add up all monthly debt payments ÷ gross monthly income. Most lenders want this under 43%. Lower is always better. Know your number before your lender tells you.
Cash Position
Know Your Real Number — Down Payment + Closing Costs
This is where first-time buyers get blindsided. Your down payment is not your total cash needed.
Closing costs typically run 2–5% of the loan amount on top of your down payment — and they're due at the closing table.

The closing cost blind spot: A buyer purchasing at $400,000 with 5% down needs $20,000 for the down payment — plus up to $20,000 more in closing costs. Budget for both before you start shopping.
02
Step 02 · Financing
GET PRE-
APPROVED,
NOT PRE-QUAL'D.
These are not the same thing. One gets you into homes. The other is just a rough guess.
Avoid This
Pre-Qualification
A rough estimate based on what you tell the lender — no credit pull, no verification. Sellers and agents know it means nothing. Don't waste time on pre-qual.
Do This
Pre-Approval
Your credit is pulled, income is verified, assets are reviewed. You receive a pre-approval letter with a specific loan amount. This is what sellers want to see.
Rate Shopping Strategy
Shop 2–3 Lenders Without Hurting Your Credit
Multiple mortgage inquiries within a 45-day window count as a single hard pull on your credit report. This is a federal rule (FICO groups mortgage inquiries). You can — and should — shop around.
1
Apply to 2–3 lenders within the same 45-day window
2
Compare Loan Estimates on the same loan type and amount
3
Look at APR, not just rate — it includes fees
4
Choose the lender with the best combination of rate + speed

Choose Your Agent Carefully
Not All Agents Know First-Time Buyers
Ask specifically about their first-time buyer experience. A good agent explains the process, sets realistic expectations, and knows how to write a strong offer — not just show houses.
Making an Offer
Your Agent Will Guide You Through This
The offer includes price, earnest money deposit, contingencies (inspection, financing, appraisal), and a proposed closing date. Your pre-approval letter accompanies every offer.
"Don't max out your pre-approval. Leave room for the taxes, insurance, HOA, and that dishwasher that breaks three weeks after you move in."

04
Step 04 · Under Contract
OFFER ACCEPTED.
NOW THE
CLOCK STARTS.
Once your offer is accepted, you have deadlines. Miss them and you could lose your earnest money.
1
Inspection Contingency (typically 7–10 days)
Hire a licensed inspector. They'll review structure, roof, systems, plumbing, electrical. The report is yours — use it to negotiate repairs or credits, or walk away if the issues are serious.
2
Formal Loan Application + Rate Lock
Go back to your lender immediately to formally apply. This is when you decide to lock your rate or float. Rate locks typically last 30–60 days — make sure it covers your closing date.
3
Appraisal (ordered by lender, typically 1–2 weeks)
Your lender orders this — you can't choose the appraiser. If the appraisal comes in below purchase price, you have options: negotiate with the seller, make up the difference in cash, or walk away.
4
Title Search + Homeowner's Insurance
Title company confirms clear ownership. Shop for homeowner's insurance now — you'll need a binder before closing. Don't wait until the week before.
⚠️
Don't waive your inspection contingency. In competitive markets, buyers feel pressure to do this. Understand what you're giving up — you lose the right to negotiate based on inspection findings or back out without losing your earnest money.

Step 05 · Financing Review
UNDERWRITING.
IT FEELS LIKE
NOTHING. IT'S NOT.
The underwriter is reviewing everything. This is the part where your behavior matters most.
What Underwriting Is
Your Entire Financial Picture Under a Microscope
The underwriter independently verifies everything your loan officer submitted — pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, employment, assets. They're looking for anything that doesn't add up.
🚫 DO NOT During Underwriting
These Can Kill Your Loan
✗
Make large purchases (car, furniture, appliances)
✗
Open new credit cards or lines of credit
✗
Change jobs or become self-employed
✗
Co-sign on anyone else's loan
✗
Move large sums of money without explanation
✓ DO During Underwriting
Stay the Course
✓
Respond to document requests immediately
✓
Keep all accounts exactly as they are
✓
Stay employed at your current job
✓
Save all financial paperwork
✓
Answer your phone — lenders need quick replies
💡
Underwriters issue "conditions" — additional items needed before final approval. Responding within hours instead of days can shave a week off your closing timeline. Be responsive.

Step 06 · Final Approval
CLEAR TO
CLOSE.
ALMOST THERE.
When underwriting is satisfied, you'll get the three most important words in homebuying.
What Clear to Close Means
"CTC" — Every Condition Has Been Satisfied
Your loan is fully approved. The lender has everything they need. Closing can be scheduled. This is the point where you start getting excited — it's real now.
The Closing Disclosure (CD)
Read Every Line — 3 Business Days Before Closing
Federal law requires lenders to provide your CD at least 3 business days before closing. It shows your final loan terms, monthly payment, and exact cash to close. Compare it to your original Loan Estimate.

Step 07 · Closing Day

DAY.
YOU OWN IT.

Do Not Bring
These Will Not Work

Personal checks (not accepted)
✗
Cash (title companies don't accept physical cash)
✗
Business checks (typically not accepted)
⚠️
Wire your funds the day before closing if possible — give it time to arrive.
What Happens at Closing
Typically 1–2 Hours of Signing
You'll sign the deed of trust, promissory note, closing disclosure acknowledgment, and a stack of other documents. The title company guides you through all of it. When the funds are disbursed and the deed is recorded — it's yours. You get the keys.

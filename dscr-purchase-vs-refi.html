DSCR Loans in an LLC: How to Finance Investment Properties Through Your Entity

SD
Sean Davoodian
NMLS # 1252107 · February 08, 2026 · 5 min read

One of the most underutilized features of DSCR loan programs is the ability to close in the name of an LLC or business entity. For serious investors, this is a critical piece of portfolio strategy — protecting personal liability and keeping business debt separate from personal credit.

Why Close in an LLC?

Personal liability protection is the primary reason. When you hold investment properties in your personal name, any lawsuit or judgment tied to those properties can reach your personal assets. An LLC creates a legal separation. Additionally, some investors prefer to keep investment debt off their personal credit report — DSCR LLC lending accomplishes both.

How Entity Vesting Works Under DSCR Guidelines

DSCR programs permit loans in the name of US domestic LLCs, limited partnerships, and corporations. The entity must be a US domestic business with activities limited to ownership and management of real estate. A maximum of four owners (members/partners/shareholders) is permitted. At least 25% of entity ownership must be represented as borrowers on the loan — each completing a separate loan application.

Required Entity Documentation

Expect to provide: Articles of Organization, Partnership, and Operating Agreements; EIN Verification Form; Certificate of Good Standing; Certificate of Authorization for the person executing documents; and a Business Purpose and Occupancy Affidavit signed by each entity owner applicant. If the entity is formed in a different state than the subject property, documentation is required for both states.

Layered LLCs

Investors with more complex entity structures can still qualify. Layered LLCs are permitted up to two layers deep. However, entities layered with a trust are not eligible. Full LLC documentation is required for all ascending entities in the ownership chain.

Personal Guarantors

Even with entity borrowing, most DSCR programs require a personal guaranty from members representing at least 51% cumulative ownership. This keeps the borrowers personally accountable while still gaining the asset protection and credit benefits of entity vesting.

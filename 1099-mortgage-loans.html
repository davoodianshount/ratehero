1099 Loans: How Independent Contractors Can Qualify for a Mortgage

SD
Sean Davoodian, NMLS #1252107
NMLS # 1252107 · February 18, 2026 · 5 min read

Independent contractors, gig workers, and commission-based salespeople face a unique mortgage challenge: W-2 income doesn't exist, and tax returns often show aggressive deductions. The 1099 loan program was built specifically for this borrower profile.

How 1099 Income Is Documented

Under the Alt Doc 1099 program, qualifying income is calculated from 1-year 1099 statements rather than tax returns. The gross income shown on the 1099 — typically box 7 (non-employee compensation) or box 1 (rents) — is used as the basis for income calculation before any expense deduction.

This is the key advantage: unlike tax returns where Schedule C deductions reduce income to its net figure, 1099-based qualification can use a portion of gross income without requiring the borrower to "prove" expenses.

1099 Restrictions

Not all 1099 income sources qualify. The income must be consistent with the borrower's stated occupation and have a reasonable expectation of continuance. Highly variable 1099 income (one unusually large year) may require averaging or further documentation. The borrower must be self-employed or an independent contractor — not an employee who received a 1099 in error.

What Documentation Is Needed

Typically: the most recent year's 1099 statement(s), a copy of the signed engagement contract or other evidence of ongoing work relationship, and potentially a business license or other evidence of self-employment. Bank statements are often also requested to validate that the 1099 income is flowing through the borrower's accounts.

1099 vs Bank Statement: Which Is Better?

Run both scenarios. Sometimes 1099 income produces a higher qualifying figure; sometimes bank statements (which capture all deposits, not just those reported to the IRS) produce more. Your loan advisor should calculate both and recommend the documentation type that maximizes your qualifying income.

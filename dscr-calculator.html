DSCR Calculator — Frequently Asked Questions

What is a DSCR calculator?

A DSCR calculator estimates whether a rental property's income is sufficient to cover its mortgage payment and operating costs. Investors use it to pre-screen deals before applying for financing. Rate Hero's calculator adds real-time rate ranges, state-specific tax and insurance estimates, and pre-approval amounts in one screen.

How is DSCR calculated?

DSCR equals monthly rental income divided by monthly PITIA (principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and association dues). A DSCR of 1.00 means the property breaks even after debt service. Anything above 1.00 means positive cash flow. Most investor lenders look for DSCR at or above 1.00 for standard pricing.

What DSCR do I need to qualify for a loan?

Most standard DSCR loan programs require a minimum DSCR of 1.00. Strong deals with DSCR above 1.20 typically receive better pricing. Rate Hero also works with low-ratio and no-ratio DSCR programs for deals that don't hit 1.00 on paper — these typically require a larger down payment or a slight rate adjustment but can still close.

Can I qualify for a DSCR loan with a ratio below 1.0?

Yes. Rate Hero works with low-ratio DSCR programs down into the 0.70s with compensating factors (larger down payment, reserves, or higher credit), and true no-ratio DSCR products when the property numbers don't pencil at all. Below that threshold, the deal can often be restructured — more equity in, a different loan product, or interest-only pricing. Call us directly if your numbers don't pencil — there's almost always a path.

What's the difference between DSCR and cap rate?

DSCR measures whether a property's income covers its debt service. Cap rate measures a property's unlevered return (net operating income divided by property value). DSCR is what a lender uses to approve the loan. Cap rate is what an investor uses to evaluate the deal independent of financing. Both matter, but they answer different questions.

How much down payment is required for a DSCR loan?

Most DSCR purchase loans require at least 20% down, with the sweet spot at 25%. Cash-out refinance on an existing rental typically maxes at 75% LTV, meaning you keep at least 25% equity in the property. First-time investor and lower-credit borrowers may see slightly higher down payment requirements.

Is DSCR based on gross rent or net rental income?

Standard DSCR loans use gross monthly rent (the actual lease amount or market rent per a rental appraisal) divided by the full PITIA payment. Operating expenses, vacancy, and property management fees are not subtracted for the DSCR calculation itself — though a smart investor factors them in when evaluating actual cash flow.

Does the DSCR calculation include property taxes and insurance?

Yes. DSCR is calculated against full PITIA — principal, interest, taxes, insurance, and any HOA dues. Leaving taxes and insurance out produces an artificially high DSCR that won't match what a lender actually approves. Rate Hero's calculator factors in state-specific tax and insurance rates automatically.

Common DSCR Mistakes Investors Make

  • Using gross rent instead of market rent. If the property is rented below market, underwriters will use the lower of the two. Budget conservatively.
  • Forgetting HOA dues in PITIA. Condos and townhomes frequently have HOA fees that sink an otherwise-qualifying DSCR. Always include them.
  • Assuming a flat 1.5% for taxes and insurance. Texas runs 2.4%. Florida insurance has tripled in some counties. Use accurate state-specific numbers — which the calculator above does automatically.
  • Not stress-testing with vacancy. A DSCR of 1.05 at full occupancy becomes 0.95 with one month of vacancy. Investors serious about cash flow model at 92–95% occupancy, not 100%.
  • Treating short-term rental income as if it's long-term. STR revenue is lumpier and seasonal. Most STR DSCR programs use 12-month trailing average or AirDNA projections, not peak-month numbers.
